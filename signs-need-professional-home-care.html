7 Signs Your Loved One May Need Professional Home Care

April 10, 2026 6 min read Health Insights
Share this article
Recognizing the signs early can make all the difference

It starts small. Your mother forgets to take her blood pressure medication. Your father seems a little unsteady on his feet. The cooking pot has a small burn mark because someone left the stove on too long. These little things can be easy to explain away.

"She's just tired today." "He's always been a bit forgetful." "It's nothing serious."

But sometimes, these small signs are actually big warnings. And waiting too long to get help can put your loved one at risk.

So how do you know when it is time to bring in professional home care? Here are seven signs that your elderly parent or relative may need more help than you can give alone.

1. Changes in Personal Hygiene

One of the earliest signs that an elderly person needs help is when they start neglecting basic hygiene. You might notice that they are wearing the same clothes for days. Their hair looks unwashed. They have body odour. Or they simply refuse to bathe.

This is not laziness. For many older adults, bathing becomes scary because they are afraid of falling on wet tiles. Or they may feel too weak to stand for that long. Sometimes, memory problems mean they simply forget.

What you can do: A professional caregiver can assist with bathing, grooming, and dressing in a way that preserves your loved one's dignity and keeps them safe.

2. Unexplained Weight Loss or Poor Eating Habits

Have you noticed that your parent has lost weight without trying? Or maybe you open their refrigerator and find expired food or almost nothing at all. Perhaps they complain that food "doesn't taste right anymore" or they have no appetite.

Weight loss in the elderly is serious. It can lead to weakness, falls, a weakened immune system, and faster decline. Sometimes the issue is physical - trouble chewing, swallowing, or digesting. Other times it is practical - they cannot shop for groceries or stand long enough to cook.

Proper nutrition is essential for maintaining strength and health in later years

What you can do: A home caregiver can prepare nutritious meals, help with feeding if needed, and ensure your loved one is eating enough throughout the day.

3. Difficulty Managing Medications

Many elderly people take multiple medications for different conditions. Managing these correctly - at the right time, in the right dose - can be complicated. Signs of trouble include:

  • Pillboxes that are still full when they should be empty
  • Missed doctor's appointments
  • Changes in behaviour that could be caused by missed or double doses
  • Hospital visits for medication-related issues

Why This Matters

Medication mistakes are one of the leading causes of hospitalization among older adults. A missed blood pressure pill can lead to a stroke. A double dose of diabetes medication can cause dangerously low blood sugar. This is not something to guess about.

What you can do: Professional caregivers are trained to remind clients about medications, check that pills have been taken, and watch for side effects or reactions.

4. Frequent Falls or Trouble Moving Around

One fall might be an accident. Two falls are a pattern. Three falls mean something is seriously wrong. Watch for:

  • New bruises on the body
  • Fear of walking or moving around the house
  • Holding onto walls or furniture for support
  • Difficulty getting up from a chair or out of bed

Falls are not a normal part of ageing. They often signal muscle weakness, balance problems, vision issues, or other medical conditions that need attention.

What you can do: A caregiver can provide mobility assistance, help with exercises to build strength, and ensure the home environment is safe. They can also be there to help if a fall does happen.

Quick Safety Check

Walk through your parent's home and look for hazards: loose rugs, poor lighting, clutter in walkways, and lack of grab bars in the bathroom. Fixing these things can prevent many falls.

5. Memory Problems That Affect Daily Life

Forgetting where you put your keys is normal. Forgetting what keys are used for is not. Watch for signs like:

  • Getting lost in familiar places (like the neighbourhood or inside their own home)
  • Asking the same question over and over again
  • Leaving the stove on or forgetting to turn off appliances
  • Missing bill payments or making unusual financial decisions
  • Difficulty following conversations or finding the right words

What you can do: Memory care requires patience and specialised skills. Professional caregivers trained in dementia care can keep your loved one engaged, safe, and as independent as possible.

6. Withdrawal From Friends, Family, and Activities

Is your parent no longer interested in things they used to love? Do they decline invitations to see grandchildren or old friends? Have they stopped attending church services or community events?

Social withdrawal can be a sign of depression, which is common among the elderly. It can also signal that the person is struggling to keep up with conversations or feeling embarrassed about their limitations. Loneliness itself is dangerous - studies show it increases the risk of heart disease, dementia, and early death.

What you can do: A caregiver provides not just physical help but also companionship. Having someone to talk to, share meals with, or go for walks with can lift mood and improve overall health.

7. Caregiver Stress - Yes, YOUR Stress Counts

Here is something many families overlook. The seventh sign is not about your loved one. It is about you. If YOU are exhausted, overwhelmed, depressed, or constantly anxious about your parent's safety, that is a sign that professional help is needed.

You cannot provide good care if you are running on empty. And your parent can feel your stress. It affects them too.

Professional caregivers work alongside families to provide the best possible care

What you can do: Bringing in professional help does not mean you have failed. It means you are smart enough to know when to ask for support. It allows you to go back to being a child, not just a caregiver. You can enjoy visits with your parent instead of feeling drained by them.

Quick Self-Assessment

If you answer YES to three or more of these, it's time to consider professional care:

  • Has your loved one had a fall in the past 6 months?
  • Do you worry about their safety when you are not there?
  • Have they lost weight without trying?
  • Are you missing work or neglecting your own family to provide care?
  • Have they forgotten to take important medications?
  • Do they seem sad, withdrawn, or anxious most days?
  • Do you feel exhausted, resentful, or guilty about caregiving?

What To Do If You See These Signs

First, don't panic. Recognising the signs is the first step toward getting help. Second, have an honest conversation with your loved one. Choose a calm moment. Use "I" statements: "I worry about you when I am not here. I would feel better if we had some extra help."

A Final Word

Deciding to bring in professional help is never easy. Many adult children feel guilty, as if they are giving up or abandoning their parent. But the opposite is true. Getting help when it is needed is an act of love. It means you want the best for your parent - and for yourself.

Your parent raised you. They took care of you when you could not take care of yourself. Now it is your turn to make sure they are safe, comfortable, and dignified. Sometimes, the best way to do that is to let a professional stand beside you.

You are not alone. Help is available. And it is okay to ask for it.
